## The Six-Blank Template

```
For [target customer]
who [statement of need or opportunity],
[product name] is a [product category]
that [statement of key benefit -- compelling reason to buy].
Unlike [primary competitive alternative],
our product [statement of primary differentiation].
```

## Fill in the blanks (with discipline)

### 1. Target customer

**Bad (generic):** "businesses", "teams", "everyone in finance"

**Good (specific):** "finance leads at 100-500 person B2B SaaS companies who close monthly"

The target should be specific enough that you can list 20-30 customers (or prospects) who match the description.

### 2. Statement of need / opportunity

**Bad (generic):** "wants to be more efficient", "needs better tools"

**Good (specific):** "loses 11 hours per close to manual reconciliation across 3+ payment processors"

The need should be measurable. If you cannot put a number on it (hours, dollars, frequency, severity), it is too abstract.

### 4. Product category

**Bad (vague):** "platform", "solution", "tool"

**Good (specific):** "payment-data reconciliation platform", "developer-first observability tool", "AI-powered customer support automation"

The category should be one a customer would search for. If no one searches for "your category", you have either invented it (good if you can lead it) or hidden in it (bad -- harder to find).

**Your product category:** [...]

---

### 5. Statement of key benefit

**Bad (generic):** "saves time", "improves productivity"

**Good (specific):** "delivers a 2-day close with full audit traceability", "reduces incident MTTR from 45 minutes to 5 minutes"

The benefit should be quantified, customer-outcome-oriented, and tied to the need.

**Your statement of key benefit:** [...]

---

### 6. Primary competitive alternative

**Bad (generic):** "other tools", "the status quo"

**Good (specific):** "spreadsheet-based reconciliation", "Datadog + PagerDuty", "Zendesk Macros"

The alternative should be the *real* alternative -- the one most customers would use if your product did not exist. For most products, the alternative is *not* a competitor SaaS -- it is a spreadsheet, an internal tool, or human labor.

**Your primary competitive alternative:** [...]

---

### 7. Primary differentiation

**Bad (generic):** "easier to use", "better experience", "more features"

**Good (specific):** "produces an audit-ready trail automatically and scales to 100K transactions per close", "explains every alert with code-level context", "integrates with every payment processor, not just one"

The differentiation should be one thing -- the *primary* one. If you list five differentiators, you do not have one.

## Test the pitch

### Test 1: Specificity

Read the pitch aloud. For each blank, ask: "could a competitor use this exact phrasing?" If yes, sharpen.

### Test 2: Memorability

Read the pitch once. Wait 1 minute. Try to repeat it. If you cannot, it is too long or too jargon-heavy.

### Test 3: Customer reaction

Show the pitch to 3 prospects. The response should be one of:

- "Yes, that's me." (Strong target fit)
- "No, that's not me." (Out-of-segment -- which is fine, the pitch is doing its job)
- "I don't understand." (BAD -- the pitch is unclear; rewrite)

If the response is "interesting, tell me more" without "that's me", the pitch is not yet activating.

### Test 4: Differentiation

Replace your product name with a competitor's name. Does the pitch still make sense? If yes, the differentiation is not yet sharp.
