# Strategic Narrative Vision Template (Andy Raskin 5-Act)

The narrative format. Suited for board decks, all-hands talks, sales pitches, fundraising.

---

**Product:** [...]
**Audience:** [Board / Sales / All-Hands / Customer Pitch / Investor]
**Date:** [YYYY-MM-DD]
**Author:** [...]
**Version:** v[X.Y]

---

## Act 1: The Undeniable Change

> A shift in the world that everyone in the audience agrees is real. Not your problem -- the customer's reality.

[1-2 paragraphs naming the change. Cite evidence: market data, regulatory shifts, technology adoption, customer behavior trends.]

**Test:** Would the audience nod and say "yes, that's true"? If they would debate it, pick a less controversial change.

---

## Act 2: The Winners and Losers

> Who benefits from this change? Who is being left behind?

**Winners (who is thriving):**

[Describe the customers / companies who have adapted. What are they doing? What does success look like?]

**Losers (who is being left behind):**

[Describe the customers / companies who are not adapting. What is happening to them? Don't be cruel -- be honest about the consequences.]

**Test:** Does the audience identify with the winners' aspirations and the losers' fears? If they are neutral, the stakes are too low.

---

## Act 3: The Promised Land

> What does the future look like for the winners? Make it emotional and concrete.

[A vivid description of the future state. Specific outcomes, specific identity, specific relief from pain.]

Examples of strong promised-land language:
- "Imagine a finance function that closes in 2 days and spends 60% of its time on forward-looking analysis."
- "Imagine a product team where every PM can prototype, every designer can ship."
- "Imagine customer support that solves problems before customers know they exist."

**Test:** Would the audience want to be in the promised land? If they shrug, it is not concrete or emotional enough.

---

## Act 4: The Obstacles

> Why is the promised land hard to reach? Without obstacles, the story has no tension.

| Obstacle | Why it blocks the promised land | How it manifests today |
|----------|----------------------------------|-------------------------|
| [Obstacle 1] | [why] | [examples] |
| [Obstacle 2] | [why] | [examples] |
| [Obstacle 3] | [why] | [examples] |

**Test:** Are the obstacles specific? "It's hard" is not an obstacle. "Existing tools assume X, but reality is Y" is an obstacle.

---

## Act 5: The Magic Gift

> Position your product as the way to overcome the obstacles. Not the only path -- the most direct path.

[1-2 paragraphs positioning the product. Specifically name how the product solves the obstacles. Tie back to the promised land.]

**Format:**

> "[Product] enables [audience] to [bridge to promised land] by [how it overcomes the obstacles]."

**Test:** Does the audience see the product as the natural answer to the obstacles? If they ask "but what about [obstacle]?", the gift is not yet complete.

---

## Closing call to action

[One or two sentences inviting the audience to join the journey. Specific next step.]

Examples:
- "If you are a finance leader who recognizes this shift, we'd like to show you how teams are making the leap."
- "If you are an engineer who wants to build the future of [category], we're hiring."
- "If you are an investor who sees this opportunity, let's talk."

---

## Narrative Quality Checklist

- [ ] Act 1 names a change the audience agrees is real
- [ ] Act 2 makes the stakes personal (winners and losers, not abstract)
- [ ] Act 3 paints a future the audience wants to live in
- [ ] Act 4 names specific, named obstacles (not generic hardship)
- [ ] Act 5 positions the product as the natural answer
- [ ] The narrative is 5-10 minutes spoken (not 30+)
- [ ] No competitor's narrative could be substituted -- the obstacles + gift are specific to this product
- [ ] Tested with 3 internal audiences -- they retained the structure and the emotional arc
