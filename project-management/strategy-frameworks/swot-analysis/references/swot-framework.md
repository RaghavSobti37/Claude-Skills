# SWOT — Framework Reference

## 1. The 2x2

|              | Helpful (positive) | Harmful (negative) |
|--------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Internal     | **Strengths**       | **Weaknesses**     |
| External     | **Opportunities**   | **Threats**        |

- **Internal** = under our control (people, money, IP, ops, brand, process)
- **External** = outside our control (market, customers, competitors, regulation, tech)

## 2. What goes where — examples

### Strengths (internal + helpful)
- Strong engineering bench in [specific area]
- Established brand in [segment]
- Proprietary dataset of [type]
- Existing customer base of [scale]
- Capital position ([runway])
- Patented IP for [tech]
- Established channel partnerships
- Geographic presence in [region]

### Weaknesses (internal + harmful)
- Tech debt slowing release cycle
- Reliance on a single major customer
- Limited international presence
- Engineering hiring slowed by location
- High churn in segment X
- Lack of enterprise-grade compliance
- Slow product-led growth motion

### Opportunities (external + helpful)
- New regulation requiring product category
- Major competitor exit / acquisition
- Distribution shift (e.g., new app store, channel)
- Technology shift (e.g., LLMs enabling new use cases)
- Customer behavior shift (post-COVID remote work, e.g.)
- Adjacent market opening up
- Currency / interest rate shift favorable

### Threats (external + harmful)
- Well-funded competitor entering
- Regulatory change reducing addressable market
- Technology shift threatening core product
- Customer concentration risk (their industry struggles)
- Geopolitical risk in major market
- Recession reducing budgets

## 3. The miscategorization test

For each item, ask: "Do we own this directly?"

| Item | Internal? | Why |
|------|-----------|-----|
| "Strong brand" | Internal | We built and own the brand |
| "Customers love us" | External | Customer behavior |
| "Talented team" | Internal | We hired them |
| "Hard to find AI talent" | External | Labor market |
| "Cash reserves" | Internal | Our balance sheet |
| "Investor appetite low" | External | Market sentiment |
| "Excellent customer support" | Internal | We operate it |
| "Customers churning to competitor" | External | Their decision (driven by external trigger) |

Common mistake: "We have great customer satisfaction" — that's customer
sentiment (external) caused by your service (internal). Pick the
underlying internal capability as the Strength.

## 4. Evidence standards

Every SWOT item should have:

- **What:** specific description (not generic)
- **Evidence:** data, customer quote, benchmark, document reference
- **Magnitude:** size, scale, or trend
- **Confidence:** how sure are we?

### Good
> "Strength: Engineering velocity 2.5x our top 3 competitors in feature
> shipping cadence — measured via product changelogs Apr 2025-Apr 2026.
> Evidence: comparative analysis (link). Magnitude: 24 features/yr vs
> 9-12. Confidence: high (measured)."

### Weak
> "Strength: Great engineering team."

The weak version could describe any company.

## 5. The TOWS matrix

After SWOT, build TOWS — the action-oriented cross-cut.

|              | Opportunities (O) | Threats (T) |
|--------------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Strengths (S)** | **SO strategies** — use strengths to capture opportunities | **ST strategies** — use strengths to defend against threats |
| **Weaknesses (W)** | **WO strategies** — address weaknesses to capture opportunities | **WT strategies** — minimize weaknesses to avoid threats |

### Example TOWS

Scope: "Entering European compliance market"

| | O1: GDPR Article 22 driving demand | T1: EU competitor with EU presence |
|---|---|---|
| **S1: Strong product** | SO: lead with product differentiation in EU marketing campaigns | ST: highlight product depth vs incumbent's older offering |
| **W1: No EU presence** | WO: hire EU-based GDPR specialist + open EU entity | WT: partner with EU systems integrator to reduce time-to-market |

Each cell yields a candidate strategy. Then prioritize.

## 6. Prioritization across TOWS cells

A typical TOWS has 16+ candidate strategies (4 cells × multiple per cell).
You can't pursue all. Apply:

- **Strategic fit:** aligns with overall strategy
- **Materiality:** worth doing (revenue, risk, brand)
- **Feasibility:** can we actually execute?
- **Timing:** does the window matter?

Pick 2-3. Resource the rest at zero or defer.

## 7. SWOT cadence

- **Annual:** at start of strategic planning
- **Quarterly:** light refresh if external environment shifts
- **Pre-strategic-event:** new market entry, fundraise, pivot, major launch

Avoid:
- SWOT every quarter mechanically (becomes ceremony)
- SWOT once + never update (becomes stale)

## 8. SWOT for a function vs company

A SWOT for "Marketing" or "Engineering" works:
- Scope = "Marketing function vs needs of FY27 strategy"
- Strengths = marketing-specific
- External = company external + cross-functional dependencies

Useful for functional planning. Don't conflate with company-level SWOT.

## 9. SWOT with bias

Common biases to surface:

- **CEO bias:** Strengths inflated; weaknesses minimized
- **Sales bias:** Opportunities expanded; threats acknowledged late
- **Engineering bias:** Tech threats over-weighted; market opportunities under-weighted
- **Founder bias:** Personal narrative ("we started this") drives Strengths
- **Recency bias:** Most-recent event dominates

Mitigations:
- External facilitator
- Anonymous input
- Pre-read of customer/competitor data
- Devil's advocate role assigned
- TOWS forces honest cross-cuts

## 10. SWOT alternatives

When SWOT is the wrong tool:

- **VRIO** (Value, Rarity, Imitability, Organization) — for sustainable competitive advantage
- **Porter's Five Forces** — for industry structure
- **PESTLE** — for macro environment
- **BCG Growth-Share Matrix** — for portfolio decisions
- **Blue Ocean Strategy Canvas** — for uncontested market space

Use SWOT for breadth. Use these others for depth on specific questions.
