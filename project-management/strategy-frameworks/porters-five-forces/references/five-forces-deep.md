# Porter's Five Forces — Deep Reference

Each force with sub-factors, examples, and scoring guidance.

## 1. Threat of New Entrants

The risk that new competitors will enter the industry, eroding incumbent margins.

### Sub-factors (each increases or decreases threat)

**Capital requirements**
- Low: software, services — high threat
- High: semiconductors, telecom — low threat

**Economies of scale**
- None: low scale advantage — high threat
- Strong: cloud platforms, networks — low threat

**Brand loyalty / switching costs**
- Low: commodities, undifferentiated services — high threat
- High: ERP systems, infrastructure software — low threat

**Access to distribution**
- Easy: digital products via app stores — high threat
- Hard: regulated industries with established channels — low threat

**Regulatory barriers**
- Low: tech, consumer — high threat
- High: banks, pharma, telecom — low threat

**Proprietary IP / tech**
- None: open source — high threat
- Strong: patents, trade secrets — low threat

**Network effects**
- Absent: niche tools — high threat
- Strong: marketplaces, social — low threat

**Expected retaliation**
- Weak: fragmented industry — high threat
- Strong: deep-pocketed incumbents — low threat

### Examples

| Industry | Threat | Why |
|----------|--------|-----|
| Restaurant chains | High | Low capital, easy entry, brand replicable |
| Commercial aircraft | Low | Massive capital, regulatory, IP, two-vendor effective monopoly |
| B2B SaaS (general) | Medium-high | Low capital but switching costs grow over time |
| Pharma | Low | Massive R&D, regulatory approval, patents |
| Quick-commerce delivery | High | Was funded → exits |

## 2. Bargaining Power of Suppliers

How much suppliers can dictate prices and terms.

### Sub-factors

**Supplier concentration**
- Many: low power
- Few (oligopoly): high power

**Substitutability of inputs**
- Many substitutes: low supplier power
- Critical inputs with no substitute: high power

**Switching cost (for you)**
- Low: easy to change vendors → low supplier power
- High: complex integration → high supplier power

**Forward integration threat**
- Supplier could replace you: high power
- Cannot: low power

**Customer importance**
- You're a large customer: low supplier power
- You're a tiny customer: high supplier power

**Differentiation of supplier offering**
- Commodity: low power
- Differentiated/branded: high power

### Examples

| Industry | Supplier power | Why |
|----------|----------------|-----|
| Smartphone manufacturing | High | Few component suppliers (Apple-grade displays, advanced chips) |
| Restaurants | Low | Many ingredient suppliers; commodity inputs |
| AI startups (foundation models) | High (2024-2026) | Few model providers; switching cost growing |
| Streaming platforms | High (content) | A few content owners (Disney, Warner) hold leverage |

## 3. Bargaining Power of Buyers

How much customers can dictate price and terms.

### Sub-factors

**Buyer concentration**
- Few large buyers: high power (enterprise sales)
- Many small buyers: low power (consumer SaaS)

**Purchase volume**
- Large per-buyer: high power
- Small per-buyer: low power

**Product differentiation**
- Undifferentiated: high buyer power (commodity)
- Differentiated: low buyer power

**Switching cost (for buyer)**
- Low: easy to leave → high buyer power
- High: deep integration → low buyer power

**Buyer information**
- Full transparency (Glassdoor, review sites): high power
- Opaque: low buyer power

**Buyer margin pressure**
- Buyers under pressure: push price down → high buyer power
- Buyers flush: low power

**Backward integration threat**
- Buyer could build instead: high power
- Cannot: low power

### Examples

| Industry | Buyer power | Why |
|----------|-------------|-----|
| Enterprise SaaS to F500 | High | Few customers, large per-deal, internal alternatives exist |
| Consumer mobile apps | Low | Millions of users; each individually low power |
| Aircraft engines | Mixed (very high per buyer) | 2-3 buyers globally; massive per-purchase |
| Search advertising | Low for SMBs | Google has near-monopoly; SMBs price-takers |

## 4. Threat of Substitutes

Risk that customers move to a different category entirely (not a competitor; a substitute).

### Substitute ≠ competitor
- Competitor: another vendor in your category
- Substitute: a different solution to the same JTBD

### Sub-factors

**Substitute price-performance**
- Substitute much cheaper: high threat
- Substitute much pricier: low threat

**Switching cost to substitute**
- Low: high threat
- High: low threat

**Buyer propensity**
- Buyers willing to substitute: high threat
- Buyers loyal to category: low threat

### Examples

| Industry | Substitute | Threat |
|----------|------------|--------|
| Movie theaters | Streaming | High (and realized) |
| Taxis | Ride-sharing | High (and realized) |
| Newspapers | Social media / aggregators | Very high |
| Premium coffee shops | Home brewing / instant | Medium |
| Traditional banks | Crypto / fintech | Medium-high |

## 5. Competitive Rivalry

How intensely existing competitors compete.

### Sub-factors

**Number of competitors**
- Many similar-sized: high rivalry
- Concentrated (oligopoly): can be lower (tacit collusion) or higher (zero-sum)

**Industry growth rate**
- Low growth: zero-sum, intense rivalry
- High growth: rising tide, less intense

**Fixed costs**
- High: pressure to fill capacity → price wars
- Low: less pressure

**Differentiation**
- Low: commodities → price competition
- High: feature competition

**Switching costs for buyers**
- Low: customers churn easily → high rivalry
- High: stickier → lower rivalry

**Exit barriers**
- High: stuck competitors fight harder
- Low: rational exits

**Strategic stakes**
- High (e.g., key tech for parent co): intense rivalry
- Low: more rational behavior

### Examples

| Industry | Rivalry | Why |
|----------|---------|-----|
| Airlines | High | High fixed costs, low differentiation, low growth |
| Soft drinks | Low | Two dominant players, brand differentiation |
| Streaming | High | Low switching, high differentiation, content wars |
| Cloud infrastructure | High | Three big players, low differentiation at base layer |
| Luxury automobiles | Medium | Brand-differentiated, healthy margins, less price-driven |

## 6. The sixth force — Complementors

Added by Porter and others later. Complementors are products/services
that increase your product's value (vs substitutes which decrease it).

**Examples:**
- Mobile OS + app developers (iOS + apps)
- Game console + game studios
- Cloud platform + SI partners
- Search engine + content publishers

**High value:** Many complementors increase market size, share value.

**Low value:** Few complementors, limited ecosystem leverage.

Complementors can become competitors (Apple making its own apps) — manage carefully.

## 7. Industry definition discipline

The most-common error: industry too broad or too narrow.

### Too broad
"Technology" — covers everything; analysis becomes generic.

### Too narrow
"Mid-market HR analytics SaaS specifically for healthcare in California" — too constrained; missing substitutes from adjacent.

### Right level
"B2B SaaS for HR analytics serving mid-market (200-2000 EE)" — specific enough to score forces meaningfully, broad enough to capture substitutes.

Test: can you name 5-10 direct competitors? If not, definition's wrong.

## 8. Time dimension

Industries change. Score forces today AND project 3-5 years out.

Common shifts:
- New regulation enters → barrier rises (decreases new entrants)
- Tech shift erodes → barrier falls (increases new entrants)
- Industry consolidation → buyer/supplier power shifts
- New substitute emerges → category boundaries shift
- AI / automation → cost structure shifts, rivalry intensifies

## 9. Combining forces

The sum of forces dictates industry attractiveness:

- **All low:** very attractive (rare; quick to attract entrants)
- **All high:** unattractive (e.g., commodity industries with strong buyers)
- **Mixed:** typical; strategy = leverage the favorable forces

The **single dominant force** usually determines the strategy. For
enterprise SaaS: buyer power often dominates. For consumer mobile:
rivalry often dominates. For pharma: new entrant barriers + regulation.

## 10. Common pitfalls

- **Force = "depends".** Take a position.
- **Industry analysis = company analysis.** Separate firm-level (SWOT) from industry-level.
- **No evidence per factor.** Just opinion dressed as analysis.
- **No strategy translation.** Score without action.
- **Ignoring complementors.** Often the most-overlooked dynamic.
- **Static snapshot.** Industries evolve; refresh.
