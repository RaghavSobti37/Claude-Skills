# Five Forces → Strategy Translation

Strategy doesn't fall out of the analysis automatically. This reference
maps each force to candidate strategic moves.

## 1. Threat of new entrants HIGH → build barriers

| Barrier to build | How |
|------------------|-----|
| **Brand** | Sustained marketing + reputation; lighthouse customers; analyst positioning |
| **Scale** | Achieve cost advantages competitors can't match (Amazon-style infra) |
| **Network effects** | Two-sided platform design; user-to-user value; viral loops |
| **Switching cost** | Deep integration; proprietary data; workflow lock-in (sticky onboarding) |
| **Regulatory** | Become deeply compliant; help shape the rules; certification moats |
| **IP** | Patents on critical processes; trade secrets; copyright; trademarks |
| **Distribution** | Lock up channels; partnerships; exclusive arrangements |
| **Talent moat** | Hire the top of a specialized field |

## 2. Supplier power HIGH → reduce dependency

| Move | When |
|------|------|
| **Diversify suppliers** | When one supplier is critical (single-vendor cloud, single chip) |
| **Standardize on commodity inputs** | When you can substitute without quality loss |
| **Backward integrate** | When supplier is extracting too much margin and you have scale to justify |
| **Long-term contracts** | When you can lock in pricing before further consolidation |
| **Help suppliers' competitors** | Promote a #2 vendor to compete with #1 |
| **Become the dominant customer** | If you can grow to where you matter |
| **Open source the layer** | If supplier is software, fund open alternative |

### Example
- OpenAI / Anthropic concentration on foundation models → strategy:
  multi-vendor adapter; open-source local models for low-stakes use cases;
  fine-tune to reduce switching cost; multi-cloud deployment.

## 3. Buyer power HIGH → reduce dependency on each

| Move | When |
|------|------|
| **Diversify customer base** | When customer concentration > 20% in one buyer |
| **Differentiate** | Reduce buyers' ability to commoditize you |
| **Raise switching costs** | Deep integrations, custom workflows, data lock-in |
| **Improve information asymmetry** | Charge for transparency they need |
| **Forward integrate** | Serve customer's customer; bypass intermediary |
| **Vertical specialization** | Become irreplaceable in specific industries |

### Example
- Enterprise SaaS with 2-3 mega-customers extracting heavy discounts →
  strategy: targeted mid-market expansion; differentiation on vertical
  features; multi-year contracts with switching cost; per-tier pricing
  with upfront commits.

## 4. Threat of substitutes HIGH → defend value prop

| Move | When |
|------|------|
| **Reframe value** | What can we deliver substitute can't? |
| **Bundle adjacent value** | Move from product → solution |
| **Lower switching cost from us to us** | Match substitute on the dimension they win on |
| **Acquire substitute** | If you have capital and they have IP |
| **Build the substitute yourself** | Cannibalize before they do (digital cameras, e.g.) |
| **Differentiate emotionally** | Brand, identity, community (e.g., Apple vs Android) |

### Example
- Movie theaters threatened by streaming → strategy: premium experience
  (IMAX, recliners, food), exclusive windows, social experience
  positioning. Some succeeded; many didn't.

## 5. Competitive rivalry HIGH → exit, niche, or consolidate

| Move | When |
|------|------|
| **Differentiate aggressively** | Standout positioning to escape commodity competition |
| **Niche down** | Pick a specific segment / vertical to dominate |
| **Acquire competitors** | If you have scale to consolidate |
| **Exit segments** | Cede unprofitable subsegments |
| **Strategic restraint** | Don't match every move; preserve margin |
| **Build switching cost** | Make customer retention harder for others to steal |
| **Vertical integrate** | Own more of the value chain |

### Example
- Airlines (chronically high rivalry) → strategy: low-cost-carrier model
  (Southwest, Ryanair); premium positioning (Singapore, Emirates);
  alliance + frequent-flyer lock-in.

## 6. The 4-quadrant strategy guide

Combine force intensity to identify strategy patterns:

| Industry pattern | Strategy |
|-------------------|----------|
| Low forces (attractive) | Defend + capture share before forces intensify |
| High new entrant + low rivalry | Build barriers fast |
| High buyer power + low rivalry | Differentiate + segment |
| High rivalry + low buyer power | Consolidate; vertical integration |
| All forces high | Exit, or find unique sub-niche |
| Forces shifting | Reposition early; first-mover advantage |

## 7. Strategy by company stage

### Startup (entering)
- Pick an industry with at least 1-2 favorable forces
- Build initial moat (network effect, switching cost, brand)
- Don't enter "all forces high" markets — most startups die there

### Growth-stage
- Reinforce the moat that worked
- Watch for force shifts (consolidation, new entrants)
- Consider expansion into adjacent industries

### Mature
- Defend moats; M&A to consolidate
- Identify substitute risk; acquire or build
- Selectively exit high-rivalry segments

### Declining
- Harvest cash flow; exit responsibly
- Don't reinvest in unfavorable industries

## 8. Translating Five Forces to OKRs

Five Forces analysis should drive specific OKRs:

| Force diagnosis | OKR theme |
|------------------|-----------|
| High buyer power | "Reduce top-10 customer revenue concentration from X to Y" |
| High rivalry | "Increase win rate vs Competitor Z from X to Y in segment A" |
| High substitute threat | "Launch [substitute-blocking feature] by Q3" |
| High new entrant threat | "Achieve [moat metric: certifications, integrations, etc.]" |
| High supplier power | "Reduce dependency on [supplier] from X% to Y%" |

If your Five Forces analysis produces no OKR delta, it was decorative.

## 9. Sustaining vs disruptive shifts

Force shifts come in two flavors:

**Sustaining** — incumbent can respond:
- New competitor in known segment
- Buyer demanding lower prices in known way
- Existing substitute getting cheaper

**Disruptive** (Christensen) — incumbent often can't respond:
- Substitute serving overshot customers at lower price (mini-mills vs integrated steel)
- New competitor with structurally different cost model (cloud vs on-prem)
- New buyer segment with different needs (low-end disruption)

Five Forces sees both. Strategy differs:
- Sustaining: incremental response
- Disruptive: structural response (often = separate org / spin-out)

## 10. Common strategy errors after Five Forces

- **Analyzing without acting.** Five Forces becomes a deck, not decisions.
- **Generic responses.** "Differentiate more" without specifics.
- **Picking too many strategic moves.** Pick 2-3.
- **Strategy without resources.** Move chosen; no headcount/$ allocated.
- **No measurement.** Strategy progress not tracked.
- **Static — done once.** Refresh annually + on major industry events.
