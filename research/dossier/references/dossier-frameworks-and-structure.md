# Dossier Frameworks & Structure

Reference for structuring dossiers by subject type + purpose.

## 1. The universal dossier skeleton

Every dossier shares this skeleton; depth varies by subject + purpose.

1. **Executive summary** — top takeaway in 5 lines max
2. **Subject overview** — basic facts
3. **Context** — environmental, historical
4. **Capabilities / assets** — what they have / can do
5. **People / leadership** — who runs / influences
6. **Performance / trajectory** — current state + trend
7. **Relationships / ecosystem** — who they're connected to
8. **Risks / open questions** — what's unclear or worrying
9. **Implications / recommendations** — so what for the reader
10. **Sources + methodology** — how we know

## 2. Company dossier specifics

### Section emphasis by purpose
- **Deal prep / partnership:** add: deal team, decision process, prior partnerships
- **Due diligence (acquisition):** add: deep financials, IP, litigation, customer contracts, employment, real estate, environmental
- **Sales pursuit:** add: buying process, competitor incumbency, technical fit
- **Investment:** add: cap table, prior rounds, unit economics, defensibility

### Standard sources
- Public filings (10-K, S-1, equivalents in jurisdiction)
- Crunchbase, PitchBook (private companies)
- LinkedIn (people + headcount trends)
- News (Bloomberg, Reuters, trade press)
- Patent databases (USPTO, EPO)
- Court records (PACER for federal US)
- Reviews (Glassdoor, G2, TrustPilot)
- Web archive (Wayback Machine for product evolution)

### Common gaps
- Real margin (often hidden in private companies)
- Customer churn data
- Equity dilution implications
- Cultural fit (hard to source externally)

## 3. Person dossier specifics

### Sections
- Background (education, prior roles)
- Current role + scope
- Public statements + positions
- Network (board, advisor seats, key relationships)
- Compensation context (if relevant)
- Areas of expertise + known weakness
- Communication style preferences
- Recent public activity
- Sensitivity flags (privacy, reputation risks)

### Standard sources
- LinkedIn
- Personal website / blog
- Twitter / X / professional social media
- Speaking engagements (conferences, podcasts)
- Authored articles / books
- News coverage
- SEC filings (if public-company executive)
- Court records
- Industry publications

### Privacy ethics
- Dossiers on people should respect privacy
- No deep-personal info unless directly relevant
- Document gathered intent, not character judgment
- For political/regulatory targets, public record only

## 4. Market dossier specifics

### Sections
- Market definition + size (TAM, SAM, SOM)
- Growth dynamics (historical, forecast, drivers)
- Customer segments
- Competitive landscape (incumbents, challengers, disruptors)
- Channel structure (direct, partner, retail)
- Regulatory environment
- Substitutes + adjacent threats
- Technology / innovation trajectory
- Pricing dynamics
- Entry barriers + economics

### Standard sources
- Industry reports (Gartner, IDC, Forrester — paid)
- Analyst reports (sell-side equities)
- Trade associations
- Government statistics (BLS, Census, Eurostat)
- Patent landscapes (R&D activity)
- Job postings (Indeed, LinkedIn — capacity signals)
- Quarterly earnings (public competitor disclosures)
- Conference content + agendas
- M&A activity (PitchBook, S&P Capital IQ)

### Common pitfalls
- Anchoring on one analyst's TAM estimate
- Confusing TAM with addressable revenue
- Ignoring channel margin economics
- Missing the regulatory shoe-to-drop

## 5. Domain / topic dossier specifics

For a specific subject area (technology, scientific field, policy area):

### Sections
- Definition + boundaries
- Historical context
- Current state of practice
- Key debates / open questions
- Leading institutions + researchers
- Funding flows
- Regulatory + policy
- Recent developments (12 months)
- Outlook + open questions

### Standard sources
- Academic literature (see `research/litreview`)
- Industry reports
- Government / NGO publications
- Conference proceedings
- Think tank publications
- Trade press
- Patent landscape (innovation signal)

## 6. Purpose-driven section emphasis

| Purpose | Front-load | Detail-heavy | De-emphasize |
|---------|-----------|--------------|--------------|
| Briefing | Executive summary, key positions | People + recent statements | History |
| Deal prep | Recommendation | Decision process + leverage | General background |
| Due diligence | Risk register | Financials + legal + IP | Marketing materials |
| Market entry | Recommendation | Market dynamics + economics | Internal company history |
| Investment | Investment thesis + risks | Unit economics + defensibility | Customer testimonials |

## 7. Length guidelines

| Purpose | Target length |
|---------|---------------|
| Pre-meeting briefing | 2-4 pages |
| Deal prep | 5-10 pages |
| Due diligence | 20-50 pages + appendices |
| Market entry | 15-30 pages |
| Investment memo | 10-20 pages |

Longer doesn't mean better. Padding hides signal.

## 8. Executive summary discipline

A useful one-page executive summary:

```
[Subject]
[Purpose / Audience]
[Date]

KEY POINTS
1. [Most important takeaway — usually a recommendation or risk]
2. [Second-most-important]
3. [Third]

CONTEXT (3-5 lines)
[Why this matters now]

RECOMMENDATION / DECISION REQUESTED
[What action the reader should take]

KEY RISKS
[Top 1-3]

OPEN QUESTIONS
[What we still need to learn]
```

Reader gets the whole story in 5 minutes; details below for those who need them.

## 9. Source documentation per section

Each section should end with sources used:

```
Sources for this section:
- [Source 1] — accessed [date], [reliability rating]
- [Source 2] — accessed [date], [reliability rating]
- [Source 3] — accessed [date], [reliability rating]
```

Lets reader verify; lets future-you refresh.

## 10. Common pitfalls

- **Dossier without executive summary.** Reader can't find the takeaway.
- **Padding to look thorough.** Dilutes the actual insight.
- **One source per claim.** Triangulate.
- **Stale dossier.** Refresh dates; document last-updated.
- **No risk surfacing.** Reader makes decision blind to risk.
- **Adversarial tone about subject.** Bias erodes trust.
- **Mixing facts + opinions without labels.** Reader can't calibrate.
- **Cite-without-read.** Errors propagate.
- **Missing methodology.** Reader can't assess trust level.
