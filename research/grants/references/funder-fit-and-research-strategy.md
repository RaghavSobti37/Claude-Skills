# Funder Fit & Research Strategy

Reference for evaluating funder fit and building a multi-year grants strategy.

## 1. The 7 fit dimensions

### Topic alignment
- Does the funder fund this research area at all?
- Is your specific topic in scope?
- Does it map to one of their active programs / RFPs?

Check: prior grants awarded; current RFPs; program officer's portfolio.

### Mechanism alignment
- R&D? services delivery? scale-up? convening? capacity building?
- Funders specialize; mismatched mechanism = guaranteed reject.

### Stage alignment
- Early-stage / seed / proof-of-concept?
- Mid-stage validation?
- Scale-up / dissemination?
- Different funders for different stages.

### Geographic alignment
- US-only? EU-only? Global? Specific country / state?
- Some funders limit by where work is conducted.

### Team profile alignment
- Academic? Nonprofit? Startup? Hybrid?
- Career-stage requirements (early-career awards)?
- Diversity goals (some explicitly funded)?

### Budget envelope alignment
- Funder's typical award size vs your need
- Below typical: looks under-ambitious
- Above typical: looks unrealistic

### Competitive density
- 5% acceptance vs 35% changes strategy entirely
- More competitive = more upfront investment to win
- Check funder's stated success rates

## 2. Funder types deep-dive

### Government (US)
- **NIH** (National Institutes of Health) — biomedical, health
- **NSF** (National Science Foundation) — basic science, engineering
- **DOE** (Department of Energy) — energy, materials, climate, computing
- **DARPA / ARPA-E / ARPA-H** — moonshot projects
- **NSF SBIR/STTR** — small business research
- **HHS** (Health & Human Services) — health programs

### Foundation (US)
- **Gates Foundation** — global health, education, US poverty
- **MacArthur** — climate, criminal justice, journalism
- **Robert Wood Johnson** — health equity
- **Ford** — social justice
- **Hewlett, Packard, Moore, Sloan** — distinct portfolios
- **Open Philanthropy** — effective-altruism-aligned
- Smaller / regional foundations

### European
- **ERC** (European Research Council) — Starting / Consolidator / Advanced grants
- **Horizon Europe** — collaborative R&D
- **National research councils** (DFG Germany, CNRS France, EPSRC UK)
- **National-level innovation agencies** (Innovate UK, Vinnova Sweden)

### Corporate
- **Big tech research grants** — Google, Microsoft, Meta, Amazon
- **Pharma research partnerships** — Pfizer, Novartis, Merck
- **Industry consortia** — domain-specific

### Crowdfunding
- **Experiment.com** — research-specific
- **Kickstarter / GoFundMe** — for public-engagement projects
- **Patreon** — ongoing support model

## 4. Building a multi-year grants strategy

A funded research program is rarely from one grant. Strategy:

### Year 1: Seed
- Small grants (SBIR Phase I, foundation seed, R21, R03)
- Generate preliminary data
- Build publications
- Strengthen team credentials

### Year 2-3: Validate
- Larger grants (R01, NSF standard, foundation main)
- Convert preliminary data to first findings
- Expand team

### Year 4-5: Sustain + diversify
- Renewal of major grants
- New direction grants
- Corporate / industry partnerships
- Commercialization (SBIR II, STTR)

Don't put all eggs in one funder. 2-4 active grants from different
funders = resilience.

## 5. Pre-submission discipline

Before drafting:

- Read 5-10 funded proposals in the program (FOIA / colleagues)
- Read the latest RFP / FOA (full text + appendices)
- Talk to the program officer (essential for major grants)
- Submit a pre-submission inquiry where allowed
- Identify potential reviewers (do you know their work?)
- Confirm your eligibility (PI requirements, citizenship, institution)

## 6. Talking to program officers

A 30-minute call with the program officer changes the trajectory of
your proposal.

### Prep
- 1-page concept summary (1-2 paragraphs problem, 1-2 paragraphs approach)
- Specific questions
- Knowledge of recent funded grants in the program

### Ask
- "Is this idea a fit for [program]?"
- "What recent grants are most similar?"
- "What aspects of the approach should I emphasize / de-emphasize?"
- "Should I pre-submit?"
- "Is there a similar funder I should also consider?"

### Don't ask
- "Will you fund me?"
- For specific feedback on a draft
- For confidential information about reviewers

## 7. The cost of applying

Common cost:
- 4-8 weeks of PI time per major proposal
- Co-investigator time
- Internal review cycle
- Compliance work (IRB, animal protocols, biosafety)
- Letters of support coordination

A proposal that takes 6 weeks of PI time costs $20-50K in opportunity
cost alone. Apply selectively.

## 8. Track record matters

Funders fund track records as much as ideas. Build:

- Publications in target journals
- Prior funding (even small)
- Speaking engagements
- Reviews of others' work (signals trust in the field)
- Collaborations across institutions
- Mentorship of trainees
- Service on grant review panels (after a few years)

A first proposal from someone with no track record needs to be exceptional.

## 9. Co-investigator strategy

Strong co-investigators:
- Bring complementary expertise
- Have prior track records in the area
- Are at recognized institutions
- Have a history of collaboration with you (or with each other)

Weak co-investigator choices:
- Adding names without specific role
- Same institution / lab as you (no expansion)
- No demonstrated collaboration
- Trainees as co-Is (usually not appropriate)

## 10. Common pitfalls

- **Applying without funder fit.** Most-cited cause of rejection.
- **Generic proposal across multiple funders.** Each has different priorities.
- **No conversation with program officer.** Misses orientation.
- **Underqualified team for scope.** Reviewer skepticism.
- **Submitting at the deadline.** Errors; missed letters.
- **Vague significance / impact.** "It's important" without specifics.
- **No risk discussion.** Naive.
- **Budget unjustified.** Red flag.
- **Last reviewer-impression weak.** Recency bias in scoring.
