# Partnership Types

Reference on the five primary partnership types — channel / reseller, OEM / embedded, tech / integration, co-marketing / strategic alliance, joint venture — with economic structure, contract patterns, KPIs, and the failure modes specific to each type.

---

## Type 1: Channel / Reseller / Distributor

**Definition:** Partner sells your product to end customers; takes a margin; may or may not add services.

**Sub-types:**
- **Authorized reseller** (transactional; standard discount)
- **VAR / Value-Added Reseller** (consultative; higher discount; adds services)
- **Distributor** (volume aggregator; sells to other resellers)
- **MSP / Managed Service Provider** (operates product on behalf of customer)

### Economic structure

- Partner discount: 15-50% off list (depending on sub-type)
- Compensation flow: customer-pays-partner; partner-pays-vendor (or vendor-pays-partner commission)
- Vendor retains the product relationship; partner owns the customer

### Contract pattern

- **Partner Agreement** (master): governs relationship; renewable annually
- **Deal addenda** per customer (often standard MSA flowed through)
- **Pricing exhibit**: discount tiers, rebate structures
- **Termination**: 90-day notice typical; transition rights for in-flight deals

### Key KPIs

- Annual partner-attributed revenue
- Partner-sourced pipeline (vs partner-influenced)
- Deal registration volume + close rate
- Average deal size by partner
- Win rate of partner deals vs direct
- Renewal rate by partner-served customer
- Partner satisfaction (NPS)

### When this works

- Geography / language coverage you can't build (local language, local support)
- Vertical expertise (regulated industries, niche markets)
- Existing customer relationships (partner has decades-long relationship with prospect)
- Implementation-heavy product where partner is the integrator

### When it fails

- Partner is multi-vendor and prioritizes whoever they're closing this month
- Vendor's pricing doesn't leave room for partner margin + healthy customer-side price
- Customer relationship owned by partner = vendor has no visibility / leverage
- Channel conflict with direct sales (overlapping deals, lost trust)

### Operational requirements

- Channel manager(s): 1 per 10-15 active partners
- Deal registration system
- Partner portal (deal reg, MDF, training, materials)
- Partner enablement program (certification, training)
- Channel ops (MDF approval, certification tracking)

## Type 2: OEM / Embedded ISV

**Definition:** Your product is embedded inside the partner's product. Customer may or may not see your brand.

**Sub-types:**
- **OEM (Original Equipment Manufacturer)**: pure white-label; partner's brand only
- **Embedded with attribution**: "Powered by [vendor]" visible to customer
- **Platform ISV**: your product runs on partner's platform (Salesforce, Microsoft, Shopify); customer sees both

### Economic structure

- Royalty model: % of partner's revenue from end customers
- OR per-instance / per-seat licensing: scaled to partner's customer base
- OR fixed annual fee for unlimited use up to cap
- ASP per end-customer: often lower than direct (you're a feature, not the whole product)
- Volume can be huge — compensates for lower per-customer ASP

### Contract pattern

- **OEM Agreement**: long-term (3-7 years)
- **Exclusivity**: partial / scoped (specific use case, specific market) common
- **Source-code escrow**: usually required by partner (mitigates risk if vendor disappears)
- **Co-development rights**: who can build on shared work?
- **Termination**: complex; long notice periods (12-24 months); transition support

### Key KPIs

- Partner end-customer count
- Royalty / revenue from OEM
- Engineering effort spent on OEM-specific work
- Partner end-customer retention (your reputation rides on partner's success)
- Quality of partner technical relationship (renewal probability)

### When this works

- Your product is a feature, not a standalone offering
- Partner's GTM is much stronger than yours in this space
- Embedded volume justifies lower per-unit price
- Partner has unique customer base you couldn't access

### When it fails

- You become disposable: partner builds the capability in-house
- Customer relationship is fully partner's: you lose direct contact for upsell / cross-sell
- Partner's product fails or stagnates: your revenue tied to their trajectory
- Quality / support issues at partner reflect on you (customer thinks it's your product)
- Royalty under-reporting: partner reports lower revenue than actual

### Operational requirements

- Strategic alliance manager (1-2 senior per major OEM partner)
- Dedicated technical support tier for partner engineering team
- Royalty tracking + quarterly audit
- Joint roadmap sessions (quarterly)
- Co-development resources (engineering)

## Type 3: Tech / Integration Partner

**Definition:** Pre-built integration between your product and theirs. Joint customers benefit from the combined solution.

**Common examples:**
- SaaS + SaaS integration (e.g., Salesforce ↔ marketing automation)
- Infrastructure + monitoring (e.g., cloud-provider integration)
- Identity + everything (SSO integrations)

### Economic structure

- Typically no direct revenue exchange
- Mutual customer-value driver
- Some tech partnerships include co-marketing budget
- Some include affiliate / referral fees for partner-sourced leads

### Contract pattern

- **Integration / Technical Partner Agreement**: relatively light
- **Marketplace listing terms** (if integration appears in either party's marketplace)
- **Trademark license**: how each can use the other's name / logo
- **Certification** / quality standards
- **Marketing rights**: case studies, joint announcements

### Key KPIs

- Customers using the integration (mutual customers)
- Integration adoption rate (% of customers that activate)
- Joint customer satisfaction
- Co-marketing campaign performance
- Influenced revenue (deals where integration was decision factor)

### When this works

- Integration removes friction for buyers in both products
- Mutual customer expansion (adding integration = more sticky)
- Joint customers visible in marketplace listings drive cross-traffic

### When it fails

- Integration built, never marketed → customers don't know about it
- Integration breaks after partner's API changes (no maintenance commitment)
- One side puts in 80% of the effort; other side coasts
- Partner integrates with your competitor next month with no advance notice

### Operational requirements

- Tech-partner manager (lighter than channel manager; can run a portfolio of 10-30)
- Engineering resources for integration build + maintenance
- Marketplace listing management
- Co-marketing program (events, content)
- Quarterly business reviews with major partners

## Type 4: Strategic Alliance / Co-marketing

**Definition:** Long-term, executive-level partnership focused on joint market positioning. Often not transactional initially; expected to enable future deals or strategic moves.

**Common examples:**
- Two companies jointly entering a new market
- Cross-promotion via events + content
- Industry coalition / consortium membership

### Economic structure

- Often no direct revenue exchange in early stages
- Shared marketing budget (co-funded campaigns)
- Mutual customer introductions
- May include later commercial structure (co-sell, co-product)

### Contract pattern

- **Strategic Alliance Agreement** (or MOU initially)
- **Governance structure**: steering committee with executive sponsors
- **Specific deliverables**: joint events per year, joint content pieces, customer wins targeted
- **Term**: 1-2 years initially; renewable
- **Termination**: typically without cause with 90-day notice

### Key KPIs

- Joint pipeline (deals influenced by alliance)
- Joint customers (mutual customers identified + activated)
- Co-marketing campaign performance
- Brand association metrics (surveys, mentions)
- Cross-introductions (partner introduces you to N target customers; you reciprocate)

### When this works

- Brands are complementary (B2B SaaS + B2B services; consumer product + retail)
- Executive sponsorship on both sides (CEO + CEO meeting quarterly)
- Specific shared goal (market entry, vertical positioning)
- Mutual benefit clear (1+1=3 visibly)

### When it fails

- All marketing, no measurable outcomes
- Sponsorship lapses (CEO who championed left; partnership drifts)
- One-sided value (you're funding the marketing; they're getting the brand lift)
- Activity ≠ outcomes (great PR; no pipeline)

### Operational requirements

- Strategic alliance manager / chief of staff role
- Quarterly executive committee meetings
- Shared marketing planning
- Joint event budget
- Co-content production (whitepapers, webinars)

## Type 5: Joint Venture / Strategic Investment

**Definition:** Two parties create a separate entity OR one invests significantly in the other. Highest commitment level.

**Sub-types:**
- **Joint Venture (JV)**: new entity with shared ownership
- **Equity investment**: one party takes equity in the other
- **Strategic acquisition** (extreme form)

### Economic structure

- Equity / ownership stakes
- Shared P&L of the JV entity
- Or: investment provides funding in exchange for board seat + commercial commitments

### Contract pattern

- **Shareholders / JV Agreement**: complex; legal-heavy
- **Investment / SAFE / Note** if equity investment
- **Commercial side-agreements** (often parallel: JV gets preferential pricing, etc.)
- **Board governance**
- **Exit clauses**: drag-along, tag-along, ROFR

### Key KPIs

- JV revenue / profitability (or investee's growth metrics)
- Strategic outcomes (market entry success, capability acquired)
- Commercial side-agreement value
- Cultural integration

### When this works

- Major strategic move (entering new geography, acquiring critical capability)
- Both parties committed to long-term outcome
- Governance and exit terms negotiated upfront

### When it fails

- Cultural mismatch
- Mismatched expectations on JV operations (one party expects autonomy; other wants control)
- Exit terms not clear → years of legal disputes
- Strategic rationale outdated (market shifted; JV no longer relevant)

### Operational requirements

- Full board governance
- Investor relations / partner management
- Often a dedicated business unit
- Legal + finance specialists
- Executive time commitment substantial

## Defensive vs offensive partnerships

Some partnerships exist specifically to block competitors or enter markets.

### Defensive partnership

- **Purpose**: prevent a competitor from getting a key relationship (the partner can't simultaneously work with you and competitor X)
- **Structure**: often exclusivity-heavy
- **Risk**: paying for exclusivity that competitor wasn't going to pursue anyway

### Market-entry partnership

- **Purpose**: enter a new geography / vertical you can't access alone
- **Structure**: partner brings the market access; you bring the product
- **Risk**: dependency on partner that's hard to unwind

### Capability partnership

- **Purpose**: acquire a capability you don't have (technology, distribution, expertise)
- **Structure**: often pre-acquisition; could become full M&A
- **Risk**: partner's capability turns out shallow; or full acquisition costs more than expected

## How types overlap in practice

Most large vendor relationships span multiple types over time:

- Tech partner → adds co-marketing → adds co-sell → becomes strategic alliance
- Reseller → adds integration → becomes specialized VAR → eventually OEM relationship
- OEM → with strategic investment → becomes deeper alliance

Plan the relationship trajectory, not just the current state.

## Killer questions per type

### Reseller / Channel

- What's their revenue contribution at year 2?
- How many other vendors do they sell?
- Who's their commercial decision-maker for our category?

### OEM

- What's the volume? Specific customers?
- What happens if we want to terminate?
- Source code escrow terms?
- Can we serve their end customers if relationship ends?

### Tech partnership

- Who maintains the integration if either side's API changes?
- Joint marketing plan and budget?
- What happens if they integrate with our direct competitor?

### Strategic alliance

- Who are the executive sponsors? Tenure?
- What specific outcomes in next 12 months?
- Governance cadence?
- Exit terms?

### JV / Strategic investment

- Ownership structure + cap table?
- Board composition + voting?
- Exit triggers + procedure?
- What happens if either company gets acquired?

## Type selection cheat sheet

| If your goal is... | Type |
|--------------------|------|
| Scale distribution in a region | Channel / Reseller |
| Reach customers you can't | Channel / Distributor |
| Embed in someone's larger platform | OEM / Embedded ISV |
| Be discoverable in someone's marketplace | Tech / Marketplace listing |
| Help customers use both products together | Tech / Integration |
| Joint market entry | Strategic Alliance |
| Block competitor relationship | Defensive Partnership |
| Acquire capability without buying company | Strategic Investment / JV |
| Test fit before acquisition | Strategic Alliance → maybe JV → maybe M&A |
