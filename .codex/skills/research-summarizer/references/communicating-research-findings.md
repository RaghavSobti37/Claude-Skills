# Communicating Research Findings

Reference for translating research synthesis into formats stakeholders
actually read and act on.

## 1. Audience first — pick the format

| Audience | Best format | Length |
|----------|-------------|--------|
| Exec leadership | One-pager / 5-slide deck | 1 page |
| Product team | Findings memo | 3-5 pages |
| Design team | Insight + journey deck | 10-15 slides |
| Engineering | Action-implications memo | 1-2 pages |
| Customer-facing teams | Quote book + themes | 5-10 pages |
| Mixed audience | Layered: 1-pager → detail | both |

Don't deliver the same artifact to all audiences. The synthesis is the
work; format is delivery.

## 2. The one-pager

The single most-impactful artifact. Structure:

```
[Title: a sentence-long takeaway]

[Question]
What we asked and why.

[Answer]
1-2 sentences with the conclusion.

[Top insights] (3-5)
- Insight 1 (evidence: 5 of 8 users; high confidence)
- Insight 2 (evidence: ...; ...)
...

[What we recommend]
- Action 1
- Action 2

[What we don't know yet]
- Open question 1
- Open question 2

[Methodology]
N=8, conducted Apr-May 2026, [segment(s)], 60-min semi-structured
interviews, [team conducting]
```

Lead with the answer. Most readers won't get past the first 200 words.

## 3. The findings memo (3-5 pages)

Layered after the one-pager:

```
1. Executive summary (the one-pager content)
2. Background and question
3. Methodology
4. Findings
   - Theme 1 with insights + evidence + quotes
   - Theme 2 ...
5. Recommendations (with priority + owner)
6. Open questions / next research
7. Appendix: participants, instruments, raw quote bank
```

## 4. The insight deck

For teams that want visual / interactive:

```
Slide 1: Title + question
Slide 2: Answer (the takeaway)
Slide 3: Methodology summary
Slide 4-8: One slide per top insight (insight + 2-3 quote evidence)
Slide 9: Themes overview (the synthesis structure)
Slide 10: Recommendations
Slide 11: Open questions
Slide 12: Appendix link / quote bank
```

## 5. The quote bank

A useful artifact for sales, marketing, support, success — and as
methodology evidence:

- Quote (verbatim, anonymized)
- Participant context (segment, role, tenure if relevant)
- Date of session
- Theme(s) tagged
- Type (positive / negative / behavioral / desire)

Useful as both back-up for synthesis and as a tool for future communication.

## 6. The journey map (when appropriate)

A journey map is overkill for many studies, but powerful when:

- Research informs end-to-end UX
- Multiple touchpoints / channels
- Cross-functional teams need shared context

Structure per stage:
- User actions
- User thoughts
- User emotions
- Pain points
- Opportunities

Don't journey-map for the sake of having one; do it when there's a
strategic UX decision at stake.

## 7. Anti-patterns in research communication

### "Insights" without recommendations
Stakeholders read insights as "interesting but not my problem."
Always: insight + implication + recommendation (or "open question").

### Recommendations without insights
Stakeholders read recommendations as "the PM's opinion."
Always: recommendation + supporting insights + evidence.

### Burying the answer
Lead with the answer. The build-up structure works for academic papers,
not stakeholder communication.

### Quote dump
A wall of quotes is data, not synthesis. Curate. Use quotes to support
themes, not to be the report.

### Pseudo-precision
"73.6% of users prefer X" — when N=11 — implies precision the data
doesn't have. Round + caveat.

### Methodology hidden in appendix
Methodology shows trust level. Surface a one-line summary up front.

### Decoration over substance
Beautiful slides with hollow content waste everyone's time.

## 8. The "research debrief" meeting

When meeting to share findings (avoid for purely informational findings —
async > meeting):

### Agenda template (45 min)
- 5 min: setup (the question, who participated, dates)
- 20 min: top insights with evidence
- 10 min: recommendations and open questions
- 10 min: Q&A and decisions

### Common dynamics
- Stakeholders want to dispute findings → ask for specific data point
- Stakeholders want different findings → check disconfirming evidence honestly
- Stakeholders want commitment to specific action → distinguish insight from decision

## 9. Member-checking — closing the loop with participants

Optional but powerful: share findings back to participants.

- Confirms (or challenges) interpretation
- Builds research relationship for future
- Increases participant trust

Cost: time. Worth it for high-stakes research; skip for routine.

## 10. The "you told us / we did" cadence

A pattern from VoC programs that also works for episodic research:

- Quarterly or per-launch summary
- "You told us X; we did Y; here's what happened"
- Builds customer trust over time
- Encourages future participation

## 11. Storing research artifacts

A modern research repository:

- Searchable (Dovetail, Condens, Notion, Coda)
- Tagged by date, persona, theme, project
- Linked to decisions made
- Includes confidence ratings on insights
- Open to anyone in the org

Hidden research compounds badly; reaccessible research compounds positively.

## 12. Negative findings — how to communicate

Sometimes research tells you something is fine, no change needed.

- Don't pad with synthetic insights to justify the research
- "This works as expected" is a legitimate finding
- Document the testing so you don't redo it
- Frame as confidence built, not negative result

## 13. Research as input vs decision

Research is input. Decisions come from product judgment + research +
business strategy.

- Researchers don't make product decisions
- PMs don't ignore inconvenient research
- The conversation between the two is the value

Frame research outputs as "what we know" + "what we recommend," not "what
must happen."

## 14. Common pitfalls

- **One artifact for all audiences.** Tailor.
- **Burying the answer.** Lead with it.
- **Pseudo-precision.** Round numbers when N is small.
- **Quote dump.** Curate.
- **Insights without implications.** Useless to readers.
- **Methodology hidden.** Surface upfront.
- **Hiding negative findings.** They build trust.
- **Static report; no follow-up.** Research compounds when revisited.
